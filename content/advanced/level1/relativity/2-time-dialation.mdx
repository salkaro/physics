## Definitions
#### Inertial Frame
- An inertial frame of reference is one which bodies move in a straight line at constant speeds

- Different frames of reference move relative to one another

#### Einstein's Postulates
- Physics laws have the same form in all inertial frames

- The speed of light in free space is invariant

#### Proper Time
- $t_0$ time for stationary observer

#### Observer at constant ($v$)
- $t$ time measured in frame of observer

#### Lorentz Factor
- $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$ where $v$ is relative velocity and $c$ is the speed of light

- Relates measurements between different inertial reference frames

- Appears in time dilation equation: $t = \gamma t_0$

- $\gamma > 1$

&nbsp;

However since the speed of light is constant, the time taken for the light to travel its respective distance, must be longer

we can calculate

$$
 l = vt, \quad d=ct_0,  \quad \sqrt{l^2 + d^2} = ct
$$

subbing in $l$ and $d$

$$
\sqrt{v^2t^2 + c^2t_0^2} = ct
$$

solving for $t$ we get

$$
t = \dfrac{t_0}{\sqrt{1 - \dfrac{v^2}{c^2}}}
$$

or rewritten as 

$$
t = \gamma t_0
$$

since 

$$
\gamma > 1
$$

then

$$
t > t_0
$$

therefore more time has passed for the observer

## Useful Equations

$$
\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}, \quad t = \gamma t_0
$$

&nbsp;

### Example 1

A spacecraft travels at $0.6c$ relative to Earth. If $10$ seconds pass on a clock aboard the spacecraft, how much time passes on Earth?

#### Answer

calculate the Lorentz factor

$$
\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{(0.6c)^2}{c^2}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.36}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.64}} = \frac{1}{0.8} = 1.25
$$

apply the time dilation formula

$$
t = \gamma t_0 = 1.25 \times 10 = 12.5 \text{ s}
$$

therefore $12.5$ seconds pass on Earth while $10$ seconds pass on the spacecraft

&nbsp;

### Example 2

A particle is accelerated to $0.95c$ in a laboratory. If the particle's internal clock measures $2.0 \times 10^{-6}$ s before it decays, how long does it appear to exist in the laboratory frame?

#### Answer

calculate the Lorentz factor

$$
\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{(0.95c)^2}{c^2}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.9025}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.0975}} \approx 3.20
$$

apply the time dilation formula

$$
t = \gamma t_0 = 3.20 \times 2.0 \times 10^{-6} = 6.4 \times 10^{-6} \text{ s}
$$

the particle appears to exist for approximately $6.4 \times 10^{-6}$ s in the laboratory frame

&nbsp;

### Example 3

A GPS satellite orbits at a speed where $\gamma = 1.0000000001$. If $1$ day passes on the satellite, how much more time passes on Earth?

#### Answer

using $t = \gamma t_0$ with $t_0 = 1$ day

$$
t = 1.0000000001 \times 1 = 1.0000000001 \text{ days}
$$

the time difference is

$$
\Delta t = t - t_0 = 1.0000000001 - 1 = 0.0000000001 \text{ days}
$$

converting to microseconds ($1$ day $= 86400$ s)

$$
\Delta t = 0.0000000001 \times 86400 \times 10^6 = 8.64 \text{ Î¼s}
$$

Earth clocks are ahead by approximately $8.64$ microseconds per day, which must be corrected for GPS accuracy
